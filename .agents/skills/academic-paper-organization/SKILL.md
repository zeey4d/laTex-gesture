# Academic Paper Organization

This skill defines the standard chronological list of components typically found in a Final Year Project (FYP) paper or thesis, organized from the very first page to the last. Adhere to this structure while drafting or reviewing academic papers and FYPs.

## Part 1: Front Matter (Preliminary Pages)
These pages are usually numbered using Roman numerals (i, ii, iii, iv...).

- **Cover Page / Title Page:** Contains the project title, your name, student ID, supervisor’s name, university/department name, degree pursued, and the date of submission.
- **Declaration of Originality:** A signed statement declaring that the work is your own and has not been plagiarized or submitted elsewhere.
- **Approval / Certification Page:** A page signed by your supervisor and/or a panel of examiners certifying that the project meets the university's standards.
- **Dedication (Optional):** A brief, personal note dedicating the work to family, friends, or loved ones.
- **Acknowledgements:** A section to thank those who supported you academically, financially, or personally.
- **Abstract:** A one-page summary of the entire project. It briefly states the problem, the methodology used, the main results, and the conclusion.
- **Table of Contents:** A sequential list of all chapters, headings, subheadings, and their corresponding page numbers.
- **List of Figures:** A list of all graphs, charts, diagrams, and images used in the document with their page numbers.
- **List of Tables:** A list of all the tables included in the document with their page numbers.
- **List of Abbreviations / Acronyms:** An alphabetical list explaining any specialized abbreviations used in your text.
- **List of Symbols (Optional):** Used mainly in engineering, math, or physics to define mathematical symbols or units of measurement.

## Part 2: The Main Body (The Chapters)
These pages are numbered using standard Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3...).

### Chapter 1: Introduction
- **Background of the Study:** Contextual information to help the reader understand why the topic is relevant.
- **Problem Statement:** The specific issue, gap, or problem your project aims to solve.
- **Research Objectives:** Clear, measurable goals of what the project will achieve.
- **Research Questions / Hypotheses:** The specific questions your project will answer.
- **Scope of the Study:** The boundaries of your project (what you will focus on and what you will not cover).
- **Significance of the Study:** Why the project matters and who will benefit from it.

### Chapter 2: Literature Review
- **Theoretical Framework:** The theories or concepts that form the basis of your work.
- **Review of Past Research:** A critical summary of existing books, journals, and papers related to your topic.
- **Research Gap:** An explanation of what is missing in current literature that your project will address.

### Chapter 3: Methodology (or Materials & Methods)
- **Research Design:** The overall strategy (e.g., qualitative, quantitative, experimental, software development life cycle).
- **Data Collection Methods:** How you gathered your data (surveys, interviews, lab experiments, sensors, etc.).
- **Tools and Materials:** Software, hardware, or materials used to build the project or analyze data.
- **Procedures:** A step-by-step explanation of how the experiment or project was conducted.

### Chapter 4: Results (or Findings)
- **Data Presentation:** Displaying the data you collected using text, charts, graphs, and tables.
- **Data Analysis:** Explaining what the data is showing (e.g., statistical analysis, software testing results, performance metrics).

### Chapter 5: Discussion
*(Note: Sometimes Results and Discussion are combined into one chapter).*
- **Interpretation of Results:** What do your findings actually mean? Did you achieve your objectives?
- **Comparison with Previous Literature:** How do your results compare to the studies you mentioned in Chapter 2?

### Chapter 6: Conclusion and Recommendations
- **Summary of Findings:** A brief wrap-up of the core discoveries or the final state of the developed product.
- **Limitations of the Study:** An honest acknowledgment of what went wrong, constraints (like time or budget), or flaws in the research.
- **Future Work / Recommendations:** Suggestions for how future students or researchers could improve upon or expand your project.

## Part 3: Back Matter (Postliminary Pages)

- **References / Bibliography:** A formatted list (in APA, IEEE, MLA, Harvard, etc.) of every single book, article, website, and paper you cited in your text.
- **Appendices (Appendix A, B, C...):** Supplementary material that is too long or detailed to fit in the main text. This can include:
  - Lengthy chunks of code
  - Blank copies of surveys or questionnaires
  - Raw data tables
  - Blueprints or complex schematics
  - Consent forms
- **Glossary (Optional):** An alphabetical dictionary of technical terms and their definitions used in your thesis.
- **Index (Optional & Rare):** An alphabetical list of keywords and the pages where they can be found.

> **Tip:** Always check the specific department's "FYP Formatting Handbook" before validating a document, as universities occasionally switch the order of the Abstract, Acknowledgements, and Dedication!
